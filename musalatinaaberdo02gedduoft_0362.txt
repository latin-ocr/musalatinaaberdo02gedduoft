10. COAST TOWNS IN FIFE.
Epigram on same by John Johnston in Dr. G. Mackenzie‘s Lives, ΙΙ. 420.

The towns, washed by the long sweep of the Forth‚ are shielded from
the blasts of Boreas by the Grampian range. With oars and sails ye skirn
Neptune's realms, dreading no Scylla or Syrtis or other terror of the deep.
Nor only do ye plough the main: ye dive into the bowels of the land of

Fergus and fetch fire from the depths of Tartarus. By your arts the* liquid
sea solidifies and solid salt emergeS. The Saxon need not boast of his rock-

hewn salt mines: the sea supplies you with ampler store. Let Scotia praise
your industries : without you the frosts of winter would be too fierce and all

her meals insipid.

10. URBES FIFAE LITTORALES.

Oppida, quae longo lavat hinc Bodotria tractu,
Flatibus a Boreae Grampius inde tegit.

Flectere vos docuit Neptuni dextera remos,
Et cava nimbosis pandere vela Notis.

Nec vos scylla vorax, nec terret vasta charybdis,
Nec vada Dulichiae quae metuere rates.

Seu syrtes tentare iuvat, seu radere cautes
Cyaneas, vobis invia nulla via est.

Nec satis est sulcare fretum, Fergusia vobis
Cogitur abstrusos pandere terra sinus. 10

Illius in gremio conclusos quaeritis ignes,
Et prope Tartarei cernitis ora Iovis.

Artibus et vestris liquidus lapidescere pontus
Cogitur, et sal fit, quod prius unda fuit.

Montibus excisas ne iactet Saxo salinas,
Largius has vobis sufficit aequor opes,

Scotia vos celebret, vestro sine munere brumae
Sunt nimis atroces, insipidaeque dapes.

11. Illius in gremio] Allusion to the coal mining industry, already notable.
13. Lapidescere.] Allusion to the salt-pans on the Fife coast.
17. Vestro sine munere] The munera are coals for winter’s fire and salt for food,

their genesis having just been described.
